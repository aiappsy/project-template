Slippage Risk

During high volatility or low liquidity, market orders can experience slippage - execution at a worse price than expected. A buy order might fill higher, or a sell order might fill lower.

Example: You try to buy at 1.1052, but due to fast movement, your order fills at 1.1055 (3 pips of slippage).

Market Order Strategy

Use market orders when you need immediate execution and are willing to accept the current spread. They're perfect for entering or exiting positions quickly without price constraints.

Limit Order Types

Buy Limit

Purpose: Buy below current price

Execution: When price drops TO or BELOW your limit

Example: EUR/USD at 1.1050, set buy limit at 1.1020

Use Case: Buying on dips

Sell Limit

Purpose: Sell above current price

Execution: When price rises TO or ABOVE your limit

Example: EUR/USD at 1.1050, set sell limit at 1.1080

Use Case: Selling on rallies

Advanced Limit Order Features

Good Till Cancelled (GTC): Order remains active until you cancel it

Good For Day (GFD): Order cancels automatically at end of trading day

Fill or Kill (FOK): Order must fill completely immediately or cancel entirely

Immediate or Cancel (IOC): Order fills whatever it can immediately, cancels the rest

Limit Order Advantages

  • Price Precision: You control exactly where you enter/exit
  • Better Pricing: Can get better prices than current market
  • Emotion Elimination: No need to watch charts constantly
  • Strategy Automation: Implements your trading plan automatically

Slippage with Stop Orders

Since stop orders become market orders when triggered, they're subject to slippage during fast markets. Your stop-loss at 1.1020 might actually fill at 1.1015 if the market is moving quickly.

This is why many traders use stop-limit orders (covered next) for more control.

When to Use Stop-Limit Orders

Ideal for:

  • Volatile markets where you want price control
  • Large position sizes where slippage could be significant
  • Trading during news events with expected volatility
  • Instruments with wide bid-ask spreads

Avoid when: You absolutely must exit a position (use regular stop instead)

The Risk of Non-Execution

The main risk with stop-limit orders is that they might not execute at all if the market gaps through your limit price. If you set a sell stop-limit with stop at 1.1020 and limit at 1.1015, and the market gaps from 1.1025 to 1.1000, your order won't execute.

  • Test OCO orders on demo account first to understand platform behavior

Trailing Stop Types

Fixed Distance

Measurement: Pips, points, or ticks

Example: 30 pip trailing stop

Behavior: Always maintains fixed distance from price

Best For: Consistent volatility environments

Percentage Based

Measurement: Percentage of price

Example: 2% trailing stop

Behavior: Distance changes with price level

Best For: Stocks with different price levels

Trailing Stop Strategies

Trend Following: Use wide trailing stops to ride major trends

Volatility Adjusted: Set stop distance based on Average True Range (ATR)

Pyramiding: Add to positions as trailing stop moves favorably

Multi-timeframe: Use different trailing stops for different timeframes in same trade

Trailing Stop Limitations

Trailing stops can be vulnerable to "whipsaws" - being stopped out by minor retracements in strong trends. They also don't protect against gap risk when markets are closed.

Some brokers don't offer trailing stops on all instruments, and they may not work during off-market hours.

When to Use Guaranteed Stops
  • High volatility events (earnings, news)
  • Weekend positions (gap risk)
  • Large position sizes
  • Low liquidity instruments

When to Avoid Guaranteed Stops
  • Day trading liquid instruments
  • Small position sizes
  • Cost-sensitive strategies

  • Normal market conditions

Broker Limitations

Not all brokers offer guaranteed stops, and those that do may have restrictions:

  • Maximum distance from current price
  • Only available on certain instruments
  • Time limitations (e.g., only for positions held overnight)
  • Minimum and maximum position sizes
  • Higher margin requirements
